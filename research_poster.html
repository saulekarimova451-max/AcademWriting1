Students' Evaluation of the Accuracy of AI-Generated Content in Academic Contexts

Saule Karimova  ·  Sanzhar Omarov  ·  Dilyara Kurmangazykyzy
Astana IT University  |  Academic Writing Course  |  Instructor: Assel Seksenbayeva

Abstract

This study investigates university students' perceptions of the accuracy of AI-generated content in academic contexts and examines the strategies used to verify it. Sixteen undergraduates completed an online survey; four participated in semi-structured interviews. While 80% regularly use AI tools, only 54% trust their factual accuracy, and 87% acknowledge that AI produces hallucinations. Four key themes emerged: AI as a starting point, misleading confident tone, fabricated citations as the hardest challenge, and a demand for structured AI literacy training. The study concludes that students rely on self-taught, inconsistent verification methods, and universities must embed AI literacy as a formal academic skill.

Introduction

  • Research Context: AI tools (e.g., ChatGPT) are widely used by university students for essay writing, research assistance, and self-study, yet they frequently generate factually incorrect or fabricated "hallucinated" content.
  • Problem Statement: Students use AI regularly but often fail to verify its accuracy; a gap between awareness of AI limitations and actual verification behaviour poses risks to academic integrity.
  • Research Aim: To investigate students' perceptions of AI content accuracy and identify their evaluation and verification strategies at Astana IT University.
  • Research Objectives:
    • Investigate perceptions of AI-generated content accuracy
    • Identify verification strategies used in academic work
    • Examine barriers that prevent consistent verification
  • RQ1: How do students perceive the accuracy of AI-generated content in academic tasks?
  • RQ2: What strategies do students use to evaluate and verify AI-generated content?

Methodology

  • Participants: 16 undergraduate students (survey); 4 purposively selected students from different institutions (interviews)
  • Research Methods: Mixed-methods — online quantitative/qualitative survey + semi-structured interviews
  • Data Collection: Microsoft Forms questionnaire + face-to-face audio-recorded interviews (3–5 min each); conducted at Astana IT University
  • Survey Details: Multiple-choice, Likert-scale, and open-ended items covering AI usage, trust, verification habits, and academic integrity
  • Interview Details: Identical questions for all participants; topics: AI usage habits, trust, verification practices, academic integrity
  • Analysis: Frequencies & percentages for survey; inductive thematic analysis for interviews; triangulation of both

Results & Findings

  • AI Tool Usage: 80% are regular users (60% "often", 20% "sometimes"); ChatGPT most used (40%), followed by Gemini (33%), Claude (20%), Copilot (7%)
  • Trust vs. Awareness Paradox: 54% trust AI factual accuracy — yet 87% agree AI produces hallucinations, and 73% agree unverified AI harms academic quality
  • Perceived Accuracy: 60% found AI "somewhat accurate", 20% "somewhat inaccurate", 13% neutral, 7% "very accurate"
  • Verification Behaviour: Only 67% consistently verify before submitting; 27% always verify, 40% often verify; 27% unknowingly submitted inaccurate AI content
  • Verification Methods: General websites (33%), textbooks/course materials (27%), academic databases (27%), cross-referencing AI tools (13%)
  • Barriers: 46.7% cite time constraints; 6.7% trust AI; 6.7% don't know how to check
  • Academic Integrity: 80% feel personally responsible for verification; 67% view unverified AI use as academic dishonesty

Discussion

  • Interpretation: A consistent gap exists between AI limitations awareness and actual verification behaviour; AI's surface-polished errors increase time cost of checking
  • Comparison with Prior Research: Aligns with Martín-Moncunill & Alonso Martínez (2025) — weak verification despite distrust; extends findings by identifying fabricated citations as the most difficult specific challenge
  • Usage Rate: 80% regular use mirrors Black & Tomlinson (2025); verification habits are self-taught, supporting Acosta-Enriquez et al. (2024) on the intention–behaviour gap
  • Implications: Without structured guidance, intention to verify does not produce consistent behaviour; universities must formalise AI literacy
  • Limitations: Small sample (n=16, single institution); self-reported data; short interviews (3–5 min) limit qualitative depth
  • Recommendations: Embed AI literacy formally; design hands-on verification workshops; provide training on academic database tools

Conclusion

  • Main Conclusion: Students demonstrate a trust–scepticism paradox: they regularly use AI tools while acknowledging hallucination risks, yet rely on informal, inconsistent verification methods
  • Key Insight: Fabricated citations are the most challenging type of hallucination to detect; AI's confident tone obscures errors and misleads users
  • Significance: As AI becomes embedded in academic practice, evaluation skills constitute a core academic competency that formal curricula must address
  • Final Recommendation: Universities should integrate structured, practice-based AI literacy programmes — including verification training and hands-on workshops — as a formal academic requirement
